College teaches you how to write essays and give presentations. The corporate world requires an entirely different communication toolkit. After 24 years in the technology industry, I have seen brilliant graduates struggle — not because of their technical skills, but because of how they communicate.

Here are five communication skills that will transform your corporate presence.

## 1. The Art of the Concise Email

Corporate inboxes are flooded. Your manager does not have time for a five-paragraph email. Master these rules:

- **Lead with the ask** — put your request or key point in the first sentence
- **Use bullet points** — break complex information into scannable chunks
- **One email, one topic** — do not bundle multiple requests together
- **Clear subject lines** — "Q3 Budget Approval Needed by Friday" beats "Question"

## 2. Speaking Up in Meetings

The biggest mistake new graduates make is staying silent in meetings. Here is how to contribute effectively:

- **Prepare beforehand** — review the agenda and come with at least one insight
- **Use the "build" technique** — "Building on what Sarah said..." shows you are listening
- **Keep it brief** — make your point in 30 seconds or less
- **Ask clarifying questions** — this shows engagement and critical thinking

## 3. Managing Up: Communicating with Your Boss

Your relationship with your manager can make or break your career. Key principles:

- **Provide solutions, not just problems** — "I noticed X issue. I suggest we do Y."
- **Give regular updates** — do not make your boss chase you for status
- **Adapt to their style** — some prefer Slack, others prefer email, some want face-to-face
- **Receive feedback gracefully** — say "Thank you for the feedback" and act on it

## 4. Cross-Team Collaboration

In corporate environments, you will work with people from different departments, each with their own priorities:

- **Understand their goals** — frame your requests in terms of how it helps them
- **Be responsive** — quick replies build trust and momentum
- **Follow through** — if you commit to something, deliver on time
- **Document agreements** — always follow up verbal discussions with a written summary

## 5. The Elevator Pitch for Your Work

You should be able to explain what you do and why it matters in 60 seconds or less:

- **Who you are** and your role
- **What you are working on** currently
- **Why it matters** to the company
- **What help you need** (if applicable)

This is invaluable in hallway conversations, town halls, and networking events.
